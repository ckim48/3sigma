    The Primate Portal Internship Program

    A joint project with Carnegie Mellon University and Rochester University of Technology to find the origin of thoughts. The Primate Portal research lab works with Seneca Park Zoo in Rochester, New York, making use of touchscreen devices and CCTV to study and record baboons' behavior.

    Research related data and raw recorded materials produced by the lab is free and accessible through their official website.

    2022 Summer 3Sigma Internship Program

    Students at this 3Sigma Internship Program provided: data visualizations, data analysis tools, software and hardware development for The Primate Portal to support and improve analysis conducted by researchers at the lab. Additionally, to facilitate the study in understanding animal minds, we implemented robotics, gamified tasks, and software features to study animal cognitive processes.

    Bio / Psychology Data Research

    Utilizing data from The Primate Portal to analyze whether there is a correlation between learned strategies and working memory.

    Design & Animation

    Create the content designs and artwork for the Primate Portal homepage, game applications.

    Full-stack App Dev.

    Development of game application to compare cognitive abilities between human children aged 5 to 8 and the baboons.

    Game Design & Game Dev.

    Design and develop games to be used at the lab study on the baboons.

    Machine Learning & AI

    Face recognition modules for identifying baboon faces, optimized for the computational resources available at the zoo.

    Reinforcement Learning

    Games that automatically adjust its level of difficulty based on baboons performances.

    Robotics

    Design and 3D print food vending machines used for rewarding baboons at the zoo.

    Software Dev.

    Develop the platforms used for data collection and data analysis at the lab.

    UI/UX & Web Dev.

    Utilize UI/UX concepts and understanding in the development stages for the various applications released by The Primary Portal.

    Bio/Psychology

    Objective

    Past studies have suggested that rhesus monkeys and tail-winding monkeys are more adaptable to situational changes than humans, and the efficiency of using learned strategies is the cause.

    The BPDR team analyzed the data received from the lab to verify this hypothesis and concluded that the hypothesis was valid only on a limited basis.
